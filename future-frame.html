FUTURE FRAME

Helping high school students to plan their future through visualization.

OVERVIEW

Future Frame is a desktop application that provides structured guidance for visualizing different aspects of students' lives, encourages freedom and creativity, and fosters connections with peers, educators, and family members.

My Role

I supported research and client liaison throughout, and I owned the end-to-end design process from ideation to delivery with a fellow designer.

Solution Highlight

IMPACT

Students experience a slight positive shift in their sense of identity.
Students are able to express more details about their future aspirations, and teachers are able to provide better guidance.

INITIAL PROBLEM

South Fayette High School is a public high school in the suburbs of Pittsburgh. They came to us with a proposal to make a course catalog for the future -- flexible, student-centered, and data-driven.

This document was thought to be the root of the problem.

It's a thick stack of paper. Students barely use it, so they don't know about a lot of the classes being offered, and it's hard for admin to make updates.

Link to document

Our client & educators had a few concerns.

Students learned about classes from word-of-mouth, which could lead to misinformation.

Some students were fixated on only taking STEM and AP classes.

Students are not interested in project-based classes that teach real-world skills.

HOWEVER, students offered a different perspective.

Word-of-mouth is more trustworthy and personalized than the course catalog.

They care about finding the right career path and taking classes that propel them forward.

Their complaints are more focused around school policy, which is out of scope for us.

We hypothesize that the root of the problem is the existing system and the growing pressure that student feel about planning their future. We can't change the system, but we can change how students and educators approach the problem.

PROBLEM REFRAME

How might we help students make informed and confident decisions regarding courses and career paths?

MAKE, TEST, AND REPEAT

To get started on this new direction, we explored ways to reshapes how students make important life decisions. Rapid prototyping led to important insights, and we decided to make a vision boarding tool.

Through trial-and-error, this was the design process that worked best for the team. It gave us a steady flow of ideas that were carefully evaluated.

VISUALIZATION IS KEY

We found it helps with articulating existing goals and interests, increasing motivation, and providing a sense of direction.

HOW IT WORKS

A vision is a vivid, picture-like mental representation of a desirable, long-term future state.

Evoked vision, which is different from merely thinking of a goal, fosters enhanced motivation, performance, and readiness for change. Furthermore, visions become more meaningful when they reflect an individual's values and identity, which reinforces commitment and progress toward a goal.

Julian Voigt, Kennon M. Sheldon, Hugo M. Kehr. When visions truly inspire: The moderating role of self-concordance in boosting positive affect, goal commitment, and goal progress. Journal of Research in Personality, Volume 109, 2024.

We're using vision boarding to help students articulate their goals and communicate with their support system (parents, teachers, peers, and so on).

Vision boards are a flexible and creative method to help adolescents articulate their goals and aspirations through visual media. They are particularly useful for those from low socioeconomic status backgrounds who may face barriers to career possibilities.

Furthermore, vision boards can help counselors support students' career and college readiness by promoting self-reflection and identity exploration.

Phillip L. Waalkes, Laura M. Gonzalez, and Crystal N. Brunson. Vision Boards and Adolescent Career Counseling: A Culturally Responsive Approach. Journal of Creativity in Mental Health, 2019.

DESIGN FOR PUBLIC HIGH SCHOOL

There are existing tools where you can make digital vision boards, such as Canva, Pinterest, and perhaps FigJam, which we used for user testing. However, these tools have two problems:

No additional guidance

Most students need guidance on finding images, thinking through different topics, and arranging their boards.

Not suited for school

Searching for images on the internet is distracting, time-consuming, and certain websites are blocked by the school.

SOLUTION

Provide guidance through flexible prompts & categories, which can be modified by educators or students themselves.

During the planning phase, students can switch between different categories.

Prompts can be checked off or skipped, and they are modified by teachers if needed.

Default prompts

Provide multiple ways of finding images -- search, explore, or upload, made possible by Pexel API.
