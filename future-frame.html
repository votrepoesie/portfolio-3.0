FUTURE FRAME

Helping high school students to plan their future through visualization.

LOGISTICS
Research + Design

I owned the end-to-end design process from ideation to delivery with a fellow designer.

In addition, I contributed greatly to research planning, qualitative data analysis, and client liaison.

PROBLEM

What do you want to do after high school?

This is the age-old question that students are facing at South Fayette High School, a public school district in the suburb of Pittsburgh.

Our clients were educators, and they were concerned with students making ill-informed decisions regarding course planning. Thus, we spent 8 months to research and design a tool that helps students make informed and confident decisions regarding courses and career paths.

And spoilers, the solution is nothing like what we and our clients expected.

SOLUTION

Future planning through visualization & the tried-and-true method of vision-boarding.

IMPACT

From sleepy & dispassionate to uncertain but hopeful

When we asked a student to describe their identity, they said sleepy and dispassionate. After 30 minutes of using the low-fidelity prototype, they felt uncertain but hopeful.

Future Frame helped them understand what they liked and why, giving them a more profound sense of self.

How did we measure impact?

Students in the driver's seat

Conversations between students and teachers is a crucial touchpoint. The mid-fidelity prototype helped most users feel more confident and articulate when sharing their goals and motivations.

Furthermore, sharing these images helped to uncover deeper aspects of a student's identity, such as cultural and personal values, making it easier for teachers to support students in more meaningful ways.

RESEARCH

We didn't make the product that clients asked for, but for a good reason.

Our clients proposed to make a course catalog that is flexible, student-centered, and data-driven. But as we found out through research, this would not have reached their goal of helping students make more well-rounded decisions regarding their future.

EDUCATORS WERE CONCERNED WITH...

Students learned about classes from word-of-mouth, which could lead to misinformation.

Some students were fixated on only taking STEM and AP classes.

Students are not interested in project-based classes that teach real-world skills.

BUT STUDENTS HAD A DIFFERENT PERSPECTIVE...

Word-of-mouth is more trustworthy and personalized than the course catalog.

They care about finding the right career path and taking classes that propel them forward.

Their complaints are more focused around school policy, which is out of scope for us.

PROBLEM REFRAME

How might we help students make better decisions regarding courses and career paths?

We hypothesize that the root of the problem is the existing system and the growing pressure that student feel about planning their future. It's much more than making a digital course catalog. We can't change the system, but we can change how students and educators approach the problem.

Through trial-and-error, this was the design process that worked best for the team. It gave us a steady flow of ideas that were carefully evaluated.

INSIGHT

Visualization is engaging and effective.

We found visualization helps with articulating existing goals and interests, increasing motivation, and providing a sense of direction. This is combined with the strength of vision boarding, which allows students to envision a desirable, long-term future state.

Evoked vision, which is different from merely thinking of a goal, fosters enhanced motivation, performance, and readiness for change.

Julian Voigt, Kennon M. Sheldon, Hugo M. Kehr. When visions truly inspire: The moderating role of self-concordance in boosting positive affect, goal commitment, and goal progress. Journal of Research in Personality, Volume 109, 2024.

Vision boards are a flexible and creative method [...] They are particularly useful for those from low socioeconomic status backgrounds who may face barriers to career possibilities.

ITERATIONS

Prioritizing user guidance + making it school-safe

There are existing tools where you can make digital vision boards, such as Canva, Pinterest, and perhaps FigJam, which we used for user testing. However, these tools have two problems:

No additional guidance

Most students need guidance on finding images, thinking through different topics, and arranging their boards.

Not suited for school

Searching for images on the internet is distracting, time-consuming, and certain websites are blocked by the school.

01
Provide guidance through flexible prompts & categories, which can be modified by educators or students themselves.

Categories were accordions, which didn't offer enough space. Another attempt was to create multiple canvases, but it results in too much clicking and confusion, and we didn't want users to spend time arranging the images during this stage.

A deliberate attempt to use the mental model of a manila folder, but it lacks flexibility. Everything still feels crowded, and there is negative space that can be eliminated.

The final design uses a different but still familiar mental model, commonly seen in drawing & design tools. Prompts and categories are differentiated from the bottom tool bar by color, because they are meant to be considered.

02
Search, explore, and upload images without leaving the app, made possible by Pexel API.
03
Yellow as the color of creativity.

Past iterations had seen green as the root color, and later, experiments with neutral yellow. Green was the branding color for SFSD, but it wasn't the right fit. Neutral yellow didn't evoke the right emotions. Eventually, I chose primary yellow as the root color, because it's vibrant, energetic, and feels to me like what creativity should be.
